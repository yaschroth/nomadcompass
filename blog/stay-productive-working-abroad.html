How to Stay Productive While Working Remotely Abroad

Marcus Chen February 2025 7 min read

Contents

  2. Build a Routine That Travels
  3. Finding Your Workspace

  7. Making It Work Long-Term

Working remotely from another country sounds like the dream, and it is, until you find yourself three hours deep into "quickly exploring the neighborhood" instead of finishing that deliverable. The freedom that makes the nomad lifestyle so appealing is the same freedom that can quietly tank your productivity if you don't build the right systems.

After two years of working as a software engineer from cities across Latin America and Southeast Asia, I've learned that productivity abroad isn't about willpower. It's about designing an environment and a set of habits that work regardless of your time zone, your apartment, or how good the street food is outside your window. Here's what actually works.

The Productivity Challenge Abroad

Let's be honest about the problem. When you work from home in your regular city, you have invisible structure: a familiar desk, a morning routine on autopilot, coworkers who expect you online at certain hours. Travel strips all of that away. Every new city means a new apartment, a new wifi password, a new coffee shop to evaluate, and a dozen new distractions competing for your attention.

The first week in a new place is almost always the least productive. You're dealing with logistics, jet lag, SIM cards, and the overwhelming urge to explore. That's normal. The mistake is thinking that first-week energy deficit will fix itself. Without deliberate systems, it won't. I've watched talented people spend months abroad while quietly falling behind on their work, not because they were lazy, but because they never adapted their workflow to the realities of constant change.

The good news? Once you build the right framework, you can actually be more productive abroad than you were at home. Fewer office distractions, more autonomy over your schedule, and the energy that comes from living somewhere genuinely exciting all work in your favor, if you channel them right.

Build a Routine That Travels

The single most impactful thing I've done for my productivity is building a morning routine that doesn't depend on location. It's simple: wake up, make coffee (or walk to the nearest cafe), do 20 minutes of planning and prioritization, then start a focused work block. Same sequence whether I'm in Chiang Mai or Medellin.

The key insight is to anchor your routine to actions, not places or times. "I work at my desk from 9 to 5" breaks the moment you change time zones or apartments. "I do my hardest task first, take a break after two hours, then handle communications" works anywhere on the planet.

The Two-Block System

I split my workday into two deep-focus blocks of 2-3 hours each, separated by a long break. The first block starts within an hour of waking up. The second block happens after lunch and exploration time. This gives me 4-6 hours of genuinely focused work daily, which is more deep work than most office workers manage, and it leaves room for the experiences that make this lifestyle worthwhile.

Morning block (high energy): Complex coding, writing, strategic thinking. No Slack, no email. Phone on airplane mode if necessary.

Afternoon block (moderate energy): Meetings, code reviews, communication, lighter tasks. This is when I'm available to my team.

The long break in between? That's for living. Go to the market. Take a walk. Eat somewhere new. This isn't slacking off; it's the recharge that makes the afternoon block possible.

Finding Your Workspace

Your workspace abroad will make or break your productivity, and the romantic notion of working from a beach bar with a laptop needs to die. You need a reliable chair, a stable internet connection, and an environment where focused work is the norm, not the exception.

The Three-Option Strategy

In every new city, I establish three workspace options within the first 48 hours:

  • Home base: Your apartment's desk or dining table. Essential for early mornings, late evenings, and days when you just need to grind. Always test the wifi speed before committing to a rental.
  • Coworking space: For days when you need structure, community, and guaranteed fast internet. A good coworking space is worth every cent of the membership fee, especially in cities where cafe wifi can be unreliable.
  • Backup cafe: A tested, laptop-friendly cafe with outlets and decent wifi. For variety and those days when you need a change of scenery without committing to the coworking commute.

Having all three means you're never stuck. Apartment wifi goes down? Head to coworking. Need a change of pace? Try the cafe. This redundancy sounds excessive until the day your building's power cuts out 30 minutes before a client call.

"The best workspace is the one you'll actually use consistently. A perfect setup you avoid is worse than a mediocre one you show up to every day."

Managing Time Zones

Time zones are the hidden tax of working abroad. They're manageable, but only if you're proactive about communication. The worst thing you can do is ignore the gap and hope your team won't notice.

The Overlap Window

Identify the 2-4 hours where your working day overlaps with your team or clients, and protect that window fiercely. This is when meetings happen, when you're responsive on Slack, and when synchronous collaboration occurs. Everything outside that window is your async deep-work time.

If you're in Southeast Asia working with a US team, that overlap might be your evening. If you're in South America working with Europeans, it's probably your morning. Neither is ideal, but knowing exactly when you need to be "on" eliminates the anxiety of feeling like you should always be available.

Async-First Communication

Push hard for async communication with your team. Write detailed updates instead of scheduling meetings. Record short Loom videos to explain complex topics. Use shared documents with clear comment threads instead of real-time brainstorming. This isn't just good for you; it's better for everyone. Teams that communicate asynchronously produce clearer, more thoughtful work.

Pro tip: Add your current time zone to your Slack status and email signature. Update it every time you move. This small act of transparency builds enormous trust with colleagues who might otherwise wonder if you're actually working or sipping coconut water on a beach.

Setting Boundaries

Boundaries go in two directions when you're abroad, and most people only think about one.

Work Boundaries: When to Stop

Without an office to leave, work can bleed into everything. The laptop is right there. The notification just needs a quick reply. Before you know it, you're responding to Slack at 10 PM from a restaurant. Define a hard stop time and honor it. Close the laptop. Put it in a bag if you have to. The work will be there tomorrow.

Life Boundaries: When to Start

The flip side is trickier. When you're in an amazing new city, it's tempting to treat every day like a vacation with some work squeezed in. But FOMO-driven exploration during work hours creates a guilt spiral: you don't fully enjoy the outing because you're thinking about work, and you don't fully focus on work because you're thinking about what you're missing.

My rule: work hours are work hours. Exploration gets evenings and weekends, plus that midday break. And I plan one dedicated "explore day" per week, usually a weekday, where work is completely off-limits. Giving yourself permission to fully explore on that day makes it much easier to fully focus on the other four.

If you're traveling with a partner or friends, communicate these boundaries explicitly. "I'm working until 2 PM, then I'm all yours" is much better than the ambiguous always-half-available state that satisfies no one.

Essential Tools & Apps

You don't need a complicated tech stack to work effectively abroad, but a few tools make a meaningful difference. Here's what I actually use every day:

Notion: My single source of truth for tasks, notes, travel logistics, and project documentation. The offline mode is critical for those moments between wifi connections. I keep a running "city setup checklist" template that I duplicate every time I arrive somewhere new.

Slack: Non-negotiable for team communication. I use aggressive notification schedules, muting everything outside my overlap window and setting custom statuses with my local time.

Loom: Replaced at least half my meetings. A 3-minute screen recording is faster to make than scheduling a call and more useful than a wall of text in a Slack thread. Especially valuable across time zones.

1Password: Managing passwords across devices and networks is a security essential. Public wifi at coworking spaces and cafes makes a password manager mandatory, not optional.

NordVPN: A VPN is non-negotiable when you're regularly connecting to unfamiliar networks. Beyond security, it's essential for accessing region-locked services and keeping your banking apps happy when they detect a foreign IP.

Beyond these core tools, keep your stack minimal. Every new app is a new thing to manage, sync, and troubleshoot when you're on shaky wifi. Master five tools rather than dabbling with fifteen.

Making It Work Long-Term

The nomads who sustain this lifestyle for years, not just months, share one trait: they treat remote work abroad as a skill to be developed, not a perk to be enjoyed. They iterate on their systems, they're honest about what isn't working, and they prioritize their professional reputation as fiercely as their travel experiences.

Start slow: If you're new to working abroad, don't jump straight to a different continent with a 12-hour time difference. Try a nearby country in a similar time zone first. Build your systems in a lower-stakes environment before testing them under pressure.

Stay longer: The productivity cost of moving to a new city is real. Every move means 2-3 days of reduced output while you settle in. Staying 4-8 weeks in one place instead of 1-2 weeks dramatically improves your average productivity.

Invest in your setup: A portable monitor, a decent pair of noise-cancelling headphones, and a travel-friendly keyboard aren't luxuries. They're tools that pay for themselves in the first week of improved focus.

Check in with yourself: Every few weeks, honestly assess whether your output matches what you'd deliver from home. If it doesn't, something in your system needs adjusting. The goal isn't to work more; it's to work effectively enough that the lifestyle is sustainable without sacrificing your career.

Working remotely abroad is one of the great privileges of the modern economy. Protecting your productivity isn't about limiting the experience; it's about ensuring you can keep doing it for as long as you want. Build the systems, respect the boundaries, and the rest takes care of itself.
