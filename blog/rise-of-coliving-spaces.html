The Rise of Coliving: Best Spaces for Digital Nomads in 2025

Coliving has gone from a niche experiment to a global movement. For digital nomads tired of the loneliness of solo apartments and the chaos of party hostels, coliving offers something in between: a private room, a built-in community, reliable WiFi, and a workspace, all under one roof. In 2025, the options are better (and more numerous) than ever.

I've stayed at more than a dozen coliving spaces across Southeast Asia, Europe, and Latin America over the past four years. Some were transformative. Others were glorified hostels charging premium rates. This guide breaks down what coliving actually is, why it's growing so fast, and which spaces are genuinely worth your money.

What Is Coliving?

At its core, coliving is shared housing designed for community. You get a private bedroom (sometimes with an ensuite bathroom), and share communal spaces like kitchens, living rooms, coworking areas, and often outdoor spaces. Most coliving operators handle utilities, cleaning, WiFi, and furnishing, so you show up with a suitcase and start living.

Think of it as the adult, professional evolution of the flat share. The key difference from a traditional rental is intentionality: coliving spaces are curated. They vet residents, organize events, and design the environment to encourage connection. The best ones feel like joining a micro-community rather than just renting a room.

For digital nomads specifically, coliving spaces typically include dedicated coworking areas with fast internet, ergonomic desks, and meeting rooms. This eliminates the need for a separate coworking membership, which can save you $150-300/month. If you've ever tried to run a Zoom call from a hostel common room, you understand why this matters.

Why Coliving Is Exploding

The numbers tell the story. The global coliving market was valued at roughly $15 billion in 2023 and is projected to surpass $30 billion by 2028. But what's driving this growth? A few converging trends:

Remote work is permanent now. The pandemic debate is over. Companies like Spotify, Airbnb, and Shopify have gone fully remote or hybrid, and millions of workers have the freedom to live wherever they want. Coliving gives these workers an instant landing pad in any city, no apartment hunting, no utility setup, no furniture shopping.

Loneliness is the nomad's real problem. Ask any experienced digital nomad what the hardest part of the lifestyle is, and it's rarely visas or time zones. It's loneliness. Coliving directly addresses this by embedding you in a community of people in the same situation. The value of having dinner companions and weekend adventure buddies shouldn't be underestimated.

Housing markets are broken. In cities like Lisbon, a top destination for digital nomads, short-term rental prices have skyrocketed. Coliving often offers better value than a furnished apartment when you factor in utilities, internet, cleaning, and coworking access.

The product has matured. Early coliving spaces were experiments. The current generation, run by well-funded operators like Selina and Outsite, offers polished experiences with professional management, curated programming, and consistent quality across locations.

Top Coliving Spaces Worldwide

After extensive personal experience and research, here are the coliving spaces I recommend for digital nomads in 2025. I've prioritized places with strong WiFi, genuine community, and fair pricing.

1. Selina

Selina is the biggest name in the coliving-for-nomads space, with locations across Latin America, Europe, and Asia. Each property blends hostel-style social vibes with private coliving rooms and dedicated coworking areas. The consistency is impressive: you know what you're getting whether you're in Lisbon, Medellín, or Bali.

Best for: Nomads who move frequently and want a reliable brand they can hop between. Locations: 100+ worldwide. Price: $600-1,500/month depending on location and room type.

2. Outsite

Outsite focuses on premium coliving in desirable locations like Lisbon, Bali, and coastal California. The spaces tend to be beautifully designed houses rather than converted hotels, which gives them a more intimate, homey feel. Their coworking setups are excellent, and the community skews slightly older and more professionally established.

Best for: Established remote workers who want comfort and quality. Locations: 30+ across Europe, Americas, and Asia. Price: $1,000-2,000/month.

3. Sun and Co (Javea, Spain)

Sun and Co is a single-location coliving that has earned a cult following among nomads. Set in a beautiful villa in the Spanish coastal town of Javea, it offers a tight-knit community, excellent coworking, and a Mediterranean lifestyle that's hard to beat. The weekly community dinners and skill-sharing sessions are legendary.

Best for: Nomads seeking a slower pace and deep community bonds. Location: Javea, Spain. Price: $800-1,200/month.

4. Roam

Roam was one of the original coliving brands, and their spaces in Bali and Miami remain top-tier. The design is stunning, apartments are fully private with kitchenettes, and the coworking areas rival dedicated offices. If you want coliving without sacrificing any personal space, Roam is your move.

Best for: Those who want privacy with optional community. Locations: Bali, Miami, Tokyo. Price: $1,500-2,500/month.

5. Tribe (Bali)

Tribe has become the go-to coliving in Bali's Canggu area, where the nomad scene is arguably the most developed in the world. It combines affordable private rooms with a lively social calendar and one of the best coworking spaces on the island. If you're heading to Bali, also check out our guide to the best coworking spaces in Bali for more workspace options.

Best for: Budget-conscious nomads who want community in Bali. Location: Canggu, Bali. Price: $500-900/month.

6. Sende (Spain & Portugal)

Sende operates in rural Galicia, Spain, offering a countryside coliving experience that's a deliberate counterpoint to the urban hustle. If you're burned out from city life, a month at Sende surrounded by forests and mountains can be genuinely restorative. The community is small (typically 10-15 residents) and deeply connected.

Best for: Nomads seeking nature, quiet, and deep work. Location: Galicia, Spain. Price: $700-1,000/month.

7. Nine Coliving (Tenerife)

Nine Coliving in Tenerife has quickly become one of Europe's most talked-about spaces. The Canary Islands offer year-round warm weather, low cost of living (compared to mainland Europe), and a growing nomad scene. Nine provides modern private rooms, solid coworking, and a social calendar packed with surf sessions, hikes, and community dinners.

Best for: Nomads who want European time zones with tropical weather. Location: Tenerife, Canary Islands. Price: $800-1,300/month.

8. Chateau Coliving (France)

Yes, you can live and work from an actual French chateau. Chateau Coliving offers month-long stays in restored chateaux in the French countryside, complete with coworking spaces, community meals, and wine tastings. It's a unique experience that proves coliving doesn't have to mean minimalist apartments.

Best for: Nomads who want a once-in-a-lifetime experience. Location: Various chateaux in France. Price: $1,200-1,800/month.

9. Outpost (Bali & Cambodia)

Outpost is a hybrid coworking-coliving brand that started in Bali and expanded to Phnom Penh. Their Canggu and Ubud locations are long-standing nomad favorites, offering excellent workspaces paired with comfortable accommodation. The Cambodian outpost is a great option for nomads looking to explore Southeast Asia on a budget, similar to our Bangkok budget guide approach.

Best for: Southeast Asia-focused nomads who prioritize workspace quality. Locations: Bali, Cambodia. Price: $600-1,200/month.

10. Anceu Coliving (Spain)

Another rural Spanish gem, Anceu operates in a restored village in Galicia, blending coliving with rural regeneration. The concept is remarkable: residents help revive a depopulated village while enjoying community living, nature, and reliable internet. It attracts a particularly thoughtful, sustainability-minded crowd.

Best for: Nomads interested in sustainability and rural living. Location: Galicia, Spain. Price: $500-800/month.

What Does Coliving Cost?

Coliving pricing varies enormously depending on location, operator, and room type. Here's a realistic breakdown of what you can expect in 2025:

Southeast Asia (Bali, Thailand, Cambodia): $500-1,200/month. This is where coliving offers the most dramatic value, you're getting a private room, coworking, community events, and often a pool for less than a basic apartment in most Western cities.

Southern Europe (Spain, Portugal, Canary Islands): $700-1,500/month. The sweet spot for many nomads. European infrastructure and quality of life at prices that won't drain your savings.

Western Europe & Americas (France, US, Mexico): $1,000-2,500/month. Premium markets with premium prices, but still competitive when you factor in what's included.

The real value calculation isn't just the sticker price. A $1,000/month coliving that includes utilities ($100), fast internet ($50), coworking ($200), cleaning ($100), and community events ($50) is effectively costing you $500 for the room alone. When you frame it that way, coliving often beats the alternatives.

Pros & Cons of Coliving

After four years of bouncing between coliving spaces, Airbnbs, and traditional rentals, here's my honest assessment:

The Pros

  • Instant community: This is the killer feature. You arrive and immediately have people to eat with, work alongside, and explore with. For nomads, this solves the biggest pain point of the lifestyle.
  • Zero setup friction: No apartment hunting, no utility accounts, no furniture shopping, no figuring out how the washing machine works. Show up, unpack, start living.
  • Built-in coworking: Having a dedicated workspace steps from your bedroom is incredibly convenient. No commute, no hunting for cafes with decent WiFi.
  • All-inclusive pricing: One bill covers everything. No surprise electricity charges or WiFi that cuts out because the landlord forgot to pay.
  • Networking opportunities: Coliving residents tend to be entrepreneurial, creative, and well-traveled. The casual conversations can lead to collaborations, friendships, and professional opportunities.

The Cons

  • Less privacy: Even with a private room, you're sharing common spaces. If you're introverted or need lots of alone time, this can be draining.
  • Variable quality: The coliving market is booming, which means plenty of subpar operators have entered the space. Do your research before booking.
  • Community dependency: Your experience is heavily influenced by who else is living there. A great cohort makes everything magical; a poor one makes it feel like an overpriced hostel.
  • Minimum stays: Most coliving spaces require at least a two-week or one-month commitment. Less flexible than a hotel or Airbnb for short stays.
  • Can feel institutional: Larger operators sometimes feel corporate and managed. The magic of coliving is organic community, and that's hard to scale.

"The best coliving experience I've had was at a 12-person space in Spain where everyone genuinely cared about the community. The worst was a 60-bed operation that was basically a hostel with a coworking room bolted on. Size matters, and smaller is almost always better."

How to Choose a Coliving Space

With hundreds of coliving spaces now operating worldwide, choosing the right one requires some homework. Here's my framework after years of trial and error:

1. Check the internet speed. This is non-negotiable for remote workers. Look for spaces that publish their speed test results (a good sign of transparency). You want at least 50 Mbps download, ideally 100+. Ask about backup connections too, one router going down shouldn't kill your workday.

2. Read recent reviews, not old ones. Coliving spaces change dramatically when management changes or when they scale up. Focus on reviews from the past 6 months. Check Google Maps, Nomad List, and Coliving.com for the most current feedback.

3. Consider the community size. Spaces with 8-20 residents tend to foster the best communities. Below 8, it can feel too small if personalities don't click. Above 30, it's hard to maintain the intimacy that makes coliving special.

4. Look at the coworking setup. A desk in the corner of a living room doesn't count. Good coliving coworking means dedicated desks, ergonomic chairs, phone booths or call rooms, and reliable power outlets. If you take video calls regularly, verify there are private spaces available.

5. Evaluate the location. A beautiful space in an inconvenient location gets old fast. Can you walk to restaurants, grocery stores, and cafes? Is there public transport nearby? Are there gyms or fitness options within reach?

6. Ask about the vibe. Some spaces skew party-heavy, others are focused on deep work and personal growth. Neither is wrong, but make sure it matches what you need. Check their social media for a sense of the culture.

7. Start with a shorter stay. If the space offers a two-week trial, take it. A month is a long time to be stuck somewhere that doesn't fit. Many operators offer weekly rates that are slightly higher per night but give you an exit option.

Is Coliving Right for You?

Coliving isn't for everyone, and that's fine. If you value complete privacy, need absolute quiet to work, or prefer to fully integrate into a local neighborhood, a traditional rental will serve you better. There's no shame in being a solo-apartment nomad.

But if you struggle with the social side of nomad life, if you're tired of the logistical overhead of setting up in a new city, or if you simply want to meet interesting people without the forced awkwardness of networking events, coliving is worth trying at least once. For many nomads, it becomes the default way to travel.

Best for: Nomads who value community, convenience, and structured social environments. Those new to the nomad lifestyle who want a soft landing. Remote workers who want reliable coworking without a separate membership. Anyone who's tired of eating dinner alone.

Not ideal for: Deep introverts who need extended alone time. Couples or families (most spaces are designed for solo travelers). Budget nomads who can find cheaper accommodation independently. Long-term residents who want to put down roots in a single neighborhood.

My advice? Start with a well-reviewed space in a city you're already interested in, commit to two weeks minimum, and go in with an open mind. The worst that happens is you get a nice room with fast WiFi. The best that happens is you find your people.
