Why Europe for Digital Nomads?

Europe remains the gold standard for digital nomads who want world-class infrastructure without sacrificing quality of life. The continent packs an absurd density of cultures, cuisines, and climates into a space you can cross in a few hours by budget airline. And in 2025, more European countries than ever are actively courting remote workers with dedicated visa programs.

But "Europe" is a big place. The experience of working remotely from a sunny Lisbon terrace is worlds apart from hunkering down in a Berlin coworking space during a grey February. Costs vary wildly too: you might spend three times as much in Zurich as you would in Tbilisi for an equivalent lifestyle.

This guide breaks down six cities that I think represent the best of what Europe offers digital nomads right now. Each one excels in different ways, and the right choice depends entirely on your priorities: budget, weather, community, nightlife, bureaucratic ease, or some combination of all of the above. If you're not sure what matters most to you, try our Nomad Taste Wheel to figure it out.

1. Lisbon, Portugal

No list of European nomad cities is complete without Lisbon. The Portuguese capital has earned its place at the top through a combination of excellent weather (300+ days of sunshine), a mature coworking ecosystem, and one of Europe's most straightforward digital nomad visa programs. The D8 visa, introduced in 2022, grants a full year of residency to remote workers who can prove a monthly income of at least EUR 3,040.

Why it's great: Lisbon hits the sweet spot between Western European quality and semi-reasonable costs. The tech scene is legitimate (Web Summit calls it home), English is widely spoken, and the food alone is worth the move. You'll find a deep, established nomad community with weekly meetups, coworking events, and enough Facebook groups to keep you informed on everything from apartment leads to the best pastel de nata in town.

Monthly cost range: EUR 1,800 - 2,500. Accommodation is the biggest variable. Central neighborhoods like Principe Real or Santos run EUR 1,200-1,800 for a one-bedroom, but moving to Arroios or Alcantara can save you several hundred euros a month.

Standout feature: The community. Lisbon's nomad network is one of the most active and welcoming in Europe. It's one of those rare cities where you can arrive knowing nobody and have a solid social circle within two weeks.

One drawback: It's not the hidden gem it once was. Rising rents and summer tourist crowds have pushed prices up significantly over the past few years. August in particular can feel overwhelmingly touristy. For the full breakdown, read our complete Lisbon digital nomad guide.

2. Budapest, Hungary

Budapest is the city that keeps overdelivering. It offers a genuinely cosmopolitan European experience at a fraction of what you'd pay in Western capitals. The architecture is stunning, the thermal bath culture is a welcome antidote to screen fatigue, and the ruin bar scene provides nightlife that's both unique and affordable.

Why it's great: The cost-to-quality ratio in Budapest is arguably the best in the EU. You get fast internet, excellent public transport, beautiful architecture, and a lively international community, all for significantly less than Lisbon or Berlin. The city also has a growing number of coworking spaces catering specifically to remote workers, and the cafe culture is superb for those who prefer working from coffee shops.

Monthly cost range: EUR 1,200 - 1,800. A well-located one-bedroom apartment in districts V, VI, or VII runs EUR 700-1,100 on a short-term lease. Eating out is remarkably affordable: a solid lunch menu at a local restaurant costs EUR 5-8.

Standout feature: The thermal baths. After a long week of Zoom calls and deadlines, soaking in the Szechenyi or Rudas baths is genuinely restorative. No other nomad city offers anything quite like it. For more detail, check our Budapest nomad guide.

One drawback: Hungarian is one of the hardest languages in Europe, and while English is common in the center and among younger Hungarians, it drops off quickly outside tourist areas. Dealing with bureaucracy (banks, utilities, government offices) without Hungarian can be frustrating.

3. Tallinn, Estonia

Tallinn is the quiet overachiever of the European nomad scene. Estonia's capital has built its reputation on digital infrastructure rather than sunshine or nightlife, and for a certain type of remote worker, that's exactly the right pitch.

Why it's great: Estonia is arguably the most digitally advanced country in Europe. The government runs almost entirely online, and their e-Residency program lets you set up and manage an EU-based business entirely remotely, even if you never set foot in the country. For freelancers and entrepreneurs who need a clean, efficient legal structure for their European clients, this is a game-changer. Tallinn itself is compact, safe, and walkable, with a fairytale Old Town and a burgeoning startup ecosystem.

Monthly cost range: EUR 1,500 - 2,200. Slightly pricier than Budapest but still well below Western European capitals. A central one-bedroom runs EUR 800-1,200. Food and transport are moderate.

Standout feature: The digital infrastructure, full stop. Free public WiFi blanketing the city, e-Residency for business formation, digital nomad visa options, and a government that genuinely understands what remote workers need. If you're building a location-independent business, Tallinn makes the administrative side almost painless.

One drawback: The winters. From November through March, Tallinn gets as few as six hours of daylight, and temperatures regularly dip below -10C. If you need sunshine to function, plan your Tallinn stint for May through September. The nomad community also thins out considerably in winter.

4. Split, Croatia

Split is the Mediterranean dream that actually works for remote workers. Croatia's second-largest city combines Adriatic coastal living, a UNESCO World Heritage core built around a Roman palace, and a cost of living that undercuts most of Southern Europe.

Why it's great: The lifestyle factor is off the charts. You can work from a cafe overlooking the sea in the morning, swim on your lunch break, and explore ancient Roman ruins in the evening. Croatia introduced a digital nomad visa in 2021 that grants a one-year stay with no local tax obligation on foreign income, which is a significant perk. The internet is reliable (Croatia invested heavily in fiber), and Split is well-connected to the rest of Europe by budget airlines.

Monthly cost range: EUR 1,400 - 2,000. Accommodation is seasonal: winter rentals can be remarkably cheap (EUR 500-800 for a one-bedroom), but summer rates spike dramatically. The sweet spot is shoulder season, April-June or September-October, when prices are reasonable and the weather is perfect.

Standout feature: Affordable coastal living in the EU. Finding a Mediterranean city with good internet, reasonable rents, and a nomad-friendly visa is harder than it sounds. Split delivers on all three, and the Dalmatian coast backdrop doesn't hurt.

One drawback: The nomad community is still developing. Split doesn't have the deep bench of coworking spaces and weekly meetups that Lisbon or Budapest do. You'll need to be more proactive about building a social circle. Summer also brings cruise ship tourists in overwhelming numbers, and the Old Town becomes nearly impassable.

5. Berlin, Germany

Berlin is the creative capital of Europe, and it attracts a particular breed of digital nomad: the one who wants edge, culture, and a city that never quite feels finished. It's messy, it's exciting, and it has one of the deepest talent pools of any city on this list.

Why it's great: Berlin's creative and tech ecosystem is unmatched. The city is home to a huge concentration of startups, freelancers, artists, and makers. Coworking spaces range from polished corporate setups to converted warehouse collectives. The cultural scene (music, art, film, food) is relentless, and the international community is enormous. English is effectively a second language in most of the city. For networking and career opportunities, no other European city comes close.

Monthly cost range: EUR 1,800 - 2,800. Berlin is no longer the bargain it was a decade ago, but it's still cheaper than London, Paris, or Munich. A one-bedroom in popular districts like Kreuzberg, Neukolln, or Friedrichshain runs EUR 1,000-1,500. The housing market is tight, though, so allow extra time for apartment hunting.

Standout feature: The creative ecosystem. Berlin attracts ambitious, unconventional people. If you're in a creative field, tech, design, writing, music, or anything that benefits from cross-pollination with other disciplines, the concentration of talent and events here is unbeatable.

One drawback: German bureaucracy is legendarily slow. Getting a tax number, registering your address (Anmeldung), or opening a bank account can involve weeks of waiting and multiple office visits. The weather is also genuinely bleak from November to March: grey, cold, and rainy. Seasonal affective disorder is common enough that it's basically a cultural touchstone.

6. Tbilisi, Georgia (Honorary Mention)

Technically straddling Europe and Asia, Tbilisi earns its spot on this list because an increasing number of European-based nomads are treating it as part of their rotation, and for good reason. Georgia's capital is one of the most affordable and welcoming cities on the nomad circuit.

Why it's great: Georgia offers visa-free entry for citizens of 95+ countries, with stays of up to one year. No visa application, no income requirements, no paperwork. Just show up. The cost of living is extraordinarily low by European standards, the food is phenomenal (Georgian cuisine is genuinely world-class), and the city has a warm, slightly chaotic energy that grows on you fast. The nomad community has exploded in recent years, with a strong showing of remote workers from across Europe and beyond.

Monthly cost range: EUR 800 - 1,300. This is not a typo. A comfortable one-bedroom in a central neighborhood like Vera or Saburtalo runs EUR 350-600. A full restaurant meal with wine costs EUR 8-15. Tbilisi might be the best value-for-money destination on this entire list. For the full story, read our Tbilisi digital nomad guide.

Standout feature: The freedom. No visa hassle, extremely low costs, incredible hospitality culture, and a city that genuinely wants you there. For nomads recovering from burnout or wanting to stretch their savings, Tbilisi is hard to beat.

One drawback: Infrastructure can be inconsistent. While central Tbilisi has good internet and modern amenities, things get patchy outside the core. The city is also not in the EU, which means no Schengen access, different banking systems, and occasional logistical friction. Getting to other European cities requires flights (no train connections to speak of), and options are more limited than from a major EU hub.

How to Choose Your City

Six cities, six very different propositions. Here's a quick framework for narrowing it down:

Prioritize budget? Tbilisi and Budapest give you the most for your money. Split is a good middle ground if you want coastal living without Lisbon prices.

Prioritize weather? Lisbon and Split are your best bets. Both offer genuine Mediterranean (or near-Mediterranean) climates with mild winters and long, sunny summers.

Prioritize career and networking? Berlin, hands down. The depth of the tech and creative ecosystem is unmatched. Lisbon is a solid second choice with its growing startup scene.

Prioritize ease of setup? Tbilisi (no visa needed for most nationalities) and Tallinn (streamlined digital infrastructure, e-Residency for business). Portugal's D8 visa is also straightforward if you meet the income threshold.

Prioritize something unique? Budapest's thermal bath culture, Tallinn's digital-first governance, Split's Roman ruins as a daily backdrop, each city has something no other place can replicate.

"The best city for you is the one that matches your current season of life. Chasing the lowest costs when you need community, or chasing nightlife when you need focus, never ends well. Be honest about what you actually need right now."

Final Thoughts

Europe in 2025 is more accessible to digital nomads than it has ever been. Dedicated visa programs, mature coworking infrastructure, and a growing cultural acceptance of remote work mean you can build a genuinely excellent life on the continent, whether you're staying for a month or settling in for years.

The six cities on this list represent a spectrum: from the sun-drenched terraces of Lisbon to the digital-first efficiency of Tallinn, from the creative chaos of Berlin to the absurd affordability of Tbilisi. None of them is perfect (show me a city that is), but each one offers something that makes it worth considering as your next base.

My advice? Pick two or three that appeal to you, spend a month in each, and see which one sticks. Europe's size works in your favor here: you can be in a completely different country in two hours on a budget flight. Take advantage of that.

Best overall pick: Lisbon, for its unmatched combination of weather, community, infrastructure, and visa options. It's more expensive than it used to be, but it's still the most complete package.

Best value pick: Tbilisi, and it's not even close. If your priority is stretching your income while living well, Georgia's capital is in a league of its own.

Best for career growth: Berlin. The networking opportunities, talent pool, and professional ecosystem make it the strongest choice for nomads who are actively building something.
